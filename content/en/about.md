Mohetios is a personal engineering workshop.

It is not only a portfolio, not only a blog, and not a company trying to look bigger than it is. It is a named space for building, writing, testing, and thinking in public.

I’m Ali Zemani, a product-minded software engineer with around 15 years of practical experience building software across web, mobile, backend systems, dashboards, internal platforms, connected products, and cloud-based workflows.

Mohetios is the name I use for my public technical identity.

It gives shape to the kind of work I care about: taking unclear ideas, unfinished notes, product questions, business rules, technical constraints, and strange early thoughts — then slowly turning them into systems that can be understood, used, maintained, and improved.

## The Name

The name begins with `Mohet`: personal, direct, close to the feeling of “I am”.

It ends with `-ios`: technical, old-sounding, almost like the name of a tool, a system, or a small school of thought.

Around the name, there are accidental echoes too. Some people hear mojito. Some may hear mojo. I like that. Not because the name comes from those words, but because the sound carries a little strangeness, a little craft, and a little quiet magic.

That quiet magic matters to me.

Not fantasy magic.  
Not hype.  
Not mystery for its own sake.

The kind of magic that happens when a vague idea becomes a working interface.  
When scattered notes become a clear architecture.  
When a small prototype becomes useful.  
When a system starts to feel alive because the pieces finally fit together.

Years ago, I used to write a sentence under the websites I designed:

Everything, in a wonderfully good way, must be.

In Persian, the original sentence is:

همه چیز به شکل شگفت‌انگیزی خوب باید باشد.

It is intentionally a little unusual. It is not a normal slogan. It is closer to a personal standard. A reminder that useful things should not only work; they should carry care, shape, honesty, and a quiet sense of quality.

That sentence still sits behind Mohetios.

## What This Place Is

Mohetios.dev is my technical notebook, personal engineering lab, and public workshop.

I use it to write about the things I build, study, and refine: product engineering, frontend architecture, full-stack web development, cloud/serverless systems, AI-assisted workflows, open-source work, connected systems, and long-term experiments around software, science, and invention.

Some pages here are polished case studies.

Some are technical notes.

Some are unfinished thoughts.

Some are records of decisions, tradeoffs, mistakes, fixes, and small proofs that may become useful later.

That is intentional.

I do not want this site to feel like a cold résumé page. I also do not want it to become a stream of random posts. Mohetios should feel like a living engineering notebook: calm, practical, readable, and honest.

A place where work can be shown while it is still becoming.

## What I Build

Most of my work sits between product thinking and engineering execution.

I like working where an idea, business process, or technical capability needs to become something usable: a dashboard, a workflow, an internal platform, a mobile app, a financial system, a monitoring interface, a cloud-connected product, or a tool that helps people do real work with less friction.

Over the years, my work has moved through many layers of software:

- CMS platforms and business websites
    
- backend APIs and data-driven systems
    
- mobile applications
    
- IoT-style and connected-product experiments
    
- real-time interfaces
    
- dashboards and internal platforms
    
- financial and operational workflows
    
- GraphQL and REST product APIs
    
- serverless and edge-oriented deployments
    
- AI-assisted product and engineering workflows
    

That path shaped how I think about software.

For me, useful software is not just frontend, backend, or infrastructure. It is a working system that connects people, data, rules, interfaces, permissions, operations, and future change.

## How I Think About Engineering

Good engineering starts before code.

It starts with understanding the flow, the constraint, the user, the business rule, and the reason the system needs to exist.

Code is only one part of the work. The harder part is shaping the system so that the pieces fit together: interface, data, API, state, permission, deployment, operation, and maintenance.

That is the kind of work I enjoy most.

Taking something unclear and making it usable.

Giving structure to complexity.

Turning ideas into systems that can actually work.

I care about software that can be understood, maintained, shipped, and improved. Technical novelty is interesting, but it only matters when it serves a real product, a real workflow, or a real human need.

The common thread is systems.

How they are designed.  
How they break.  
How they become useful.  
How ideas unfold into working things.

## Why I Write

I write because building software leaves behind more than code.

There are decisions.  
There are tradeoffs.  
There are constraints that shaped the system.  
There are mistakes that taught something.  
There are small patterns that become useful only after they are written down.

Mohetios is where I keep those things visible.

Not every note needs to be perfect. Not every experiment needs to become a product. Not every idea needs to be finished before it can be useful.

Sometimes the value is in showing the path.

## The Standard

Mohetios is not about perfection.

It is about care.

Care in how things are named.  
Care in how systems are shaped.  
Care in how interfaces behave.  
Care in how tradeoffs are explained.  
Care in how unfinished work is allowed to become better.

The goal is simple:

Build small proofs.  
Write down the tradeoffs.  
Let ideas stay visible long enough to become useful.  
Turn vague thoughts into working systems.  
Keep the process open enough that others can learn from it.

Mohetios is where ideas unfold into systems.

And behind all of it, the old sentence remains:

Everything, in a wonderfully good way, must be.
